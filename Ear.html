Conductive Hearing Loss

Conductive hearing loss results when there is any problem in delivering sound energy to your cochlea, the hearing part in the inner ear.

Earwax (Cerumen Impaction)

Earwax, called cerumen, is produced by special wax-forming glands located in the skin of the outer one-third of the ear canal.

Tinnitus

Over 50 million Americans have experienced tinnitus, or ringing in ears, which is the perception of sound without an external source being present.

Understanding the Structure of the Human Ear

Parts of the Human Ear

Learn More About Each Part

The External Ear

The external ear consists of the pinna (or auricle) and the external auditory canal. The pinna is the visible part of the ear, made of cartilage and skin, which helps to capture sound waves and funnel them into the ear canal. The canal is a tube-like structure that leads to the eardrum (tympanic membrane). It also produces earwax to protect the ear from dust, debris, and infections.

The Middle Ear

The middle ear is an air-filled cavity that lies between the eardrum and the inner ear. It contains three tiny bones called ossicles: the malleus (hammer), incus (anvil), and stapes (stirrup). These bones form a chain that transmits and amplifies sound vibrations from the eardrum to the inner ear. The middle ear also connects to the throat via the Eustachian tube, which helps regulate air pressure.

The Inner Ear

The inner ear is a complex structure responsible for both hearing and balance. The cochlea, a spiral-shaped organ, converts sound vibrations into electrical signals that are sent to the brain via the auditory nerve. The vestibular system, which includes the semicircular canals, utricle, and saccule, helps maintain balance and spatial orientation by detecting head movements and gravity.

The Eustachian (Auditory) Tube

The Eustachian tube is a narrow passage that connects the middle ear to the nasopharynx (the upper part of the throat). Its primary functions are to equalize air pressure on both sides of the eardrum and to drain mucus or fluids from the middle ear. This tube typically opens when you yawn, swallow, or chew, helping to prevent pressure buildup that can cause discomfort or hearing issues.

Tips for Maintaining Ear Health

Avoid Loud Noises

Prolonged exposure to loud sounds can damage your hearing. Use ear protection in noisy environments.

Keep Ears Dry

Moisture in the ear canal can lead to infections. Dry your ears thoroughly after swimming or bathing.

Don’t Use Cotton Swabs

Inserting objects like cotton swabs can push earwax deeper and damage the eardrum.

Regular Checkups

Visit an audiologist or ENT specialist for regular ear examinations to catch issues early.

Manage Allergies

Allergies can cause Eustachian tube dysfunction. Treat allergies promptly to avoid ear pressure issues.

Practice Good Hygiene

Wash your hands regularly to prevent infections that can affect the ears.

Avoid Smoking

Smoking can irritate the Eustachian tube and increase the risk of ear infections.

Stay Hydrated

Proper hydration helps maintain healthy mucus production in the ear and throat.

Protect During Flights

Chew gum or yawn during takeoff and landing to help equalize ear pressure.

Monitor Earwax Buildup

Excess earwax can cause hearing loss or discomfort. Seek professional removal if needed.
