What is the nose?

Your nose, a structure that sticks out from the middle of your face, is the entrance to your respiratory system. It warms, conditions and filters the air you breathe. It also houses your olfactory organs, which give you your sense of smell.

Words that start with “naso-” (like nasopharyngeal) or “rhino-” (like rhinoplasty) usually refer to your nose. And if you develop a health condition that affects your nose, you’ll likely visit an otolaryngologist (ENT).

Function

What does your nose do?

The human nose has many important jobs related to breathing and to smell. Your nose:

  • • Filters the air: Your nose traps dust, pollen, and other particles with its hairs and mucus, preventing them from entering your lungs.
  • • Warms and humidifies: The nasal passages add warmth and moisture to the air you breathe, making it easier for your lungs to process.
  • • Smell detection: Olfactory cells in your nose detect odors, sending signals to your brain to identify smells, which also enhances your sense of taste.
  • • Resonates sound: Your nasal cavity helps with voice resonance, contributing to the tone and quality of your speech.
  • • Immune defense: The mucus in your nose contains enzymes and antibodies that fight off germs, acting as a first line of defense for your respiratory system.

These functions work together to protect your health and enhance your sensory experience, making the nose a vital part of daily life.

Hand

Hand

What are the parts of the hand?

The hand consists of bones, muscles, tendons, and other tissues that enable movement and dexterity. The visible parts include:

  • • Palm: The flat, inner surface of the hand that provides grip and support.
  • • Fingers: Five digits (thumb, index, middle, ring, and pinky) that allow for fine motor skills.
  • • Thumb: An opposable digit that enhances grasping and manipulation.

The internal structures of the hand include:

  • • Carpals: The wrist bones that form the base of the hand and provide stability.
  • • Metacarpals: The long bones in the palm that connect the carpals to the fingers.
  • • Phalanges: The bones within the fingers and thumb, allowing for flexibility.
  • • Tendons: Connect muscles to bones, enabling hand movements.
  • • Nerves: Provide sensation and control to the hand, such as the median and ulnar nerves.

Conditions and Disorders

What are the common conditions and disorders that affect your skin?

Various conditions and disorders can impact your skin, affecting its appearance and health. Some common ones include:

  • • Eczema (Atopic Dermatitis): A chronic condition causing red, itchy, and inflamed skin, often triggered by allergens or stress.
  • • Acne: A common condition involving clogged pores, leading to pimples, blackheads, and sometimes cysts, often linked to hormones.
  • • Psoriasis: An autoimmune disorder causing rapid skin cell turnover, resulting in scaly patches that can be itchy or painful.
  • • Rosacea: A chronic condition causing facial redness, visible blood vessels, and sometimes pimples, often worsened by triggers like sun exposure.
  • • Skin Cancer: Abnormal growths that can develop from sun exposure or genetic factors, requiring early detection and treatment.

If you notice persistent or unusual skin changes, consult a dermatologist for a thorough evaluation and appropriate care.

Eye Care

How can I maintain healthy eyes?

Taking care of your eyes is essential for good vision and overall health. Here are some practical tips to keep your eyes in top condition:

  • • Wear sunglasses: Protect your eyes from UV rays with polarized sunglasses that block 100% of UVA and UVB rays.
  • • Follow the 20-20-20 rule: Every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds to reduce eye strain from screens.
  • • Eat eye-healthy foods: Include leafy greens, carrots, and fish rich in omega-3 fatty acids to support vision.
  • • Stay hydrated: Drink plenty of water to prevent dry eyes and maintain moisture.
  • • Avoid rubbing your eyes: This can introduce germs and cause irritation or infections.

When should I consult an eye doctor?

Seek professional advice if you notice any of the following:

  • • Sudden vision changes: Blurred vision, double vision, or loss of vision requires immediate attention.
  • • Persistent redness or pain: This could indicate an infection or other serious condition.
  • • Frequent floaters or flashes: These may signal retinal issues that need evaluation.
